# Cultivate a Project Direction

Use this path before a continuing product, publication, or artifact family has
an inherited visual direction. Do not run it for every design task. If a
grounded direction or design system already exists, inspect and use that source
instead.

1. Recover the project's purpose, audiences, history, existing artifacts,
   material constraints, and decisions that visual form must enable. Separate
   declared identity from personal taste and from current implementation
   accidents.
2. When a named style cue or expressive cross-surface relation materially
   shapes the project, read `references/visual-language.md` before retaining
   sources. Use its formal lenses to expose different readings without turning
   the source field into a style catalog. Then assemble a small **source field**
   by role rather than fame or visual similarity:
   - inherited project work and accepted/rejected human preference evidence;
   - direct exemplars that perform a comparable job well;
   - adjacent exemplars whose hierarchy, rhythm, materiality, interaction, or
     public relation could change a decision here;
   - cultural or historical sources that give the project a deeper visual
     language than a current product trend; and
   - at least one attractive counterexample that clarifies what would be wrong
     for this project.

   Prefer primary artifacts and official source material. Qualify a source by
   the dimension in which it is actually good: observed usefulness,
   accessibility, formal clarity, cultural durability, technical invention,
   or explicit human preference. Retain it only when the artifact is
   inspectable, that relevant merit has evidence or a named nominator, the
   decision contrast is material here, and the transfer limit can be stated.
   Awards, popularity, novelty, or resemblance alone do not qualify it. A
   source field is not a ranking, screenshot dump, or license to copy.
   If the project lacks enough inspectable visual contrast, read
   `references/visual-seeds/index.md`, select two cards that expose different
   relations, and inspect their live artifacts. Add another only in a later
   iteration when human feedback or a real fragment reveals a missing material
   contrast. If formation,
   maintenance, or system evidence remains missing, additionally use
   `references/design-sources/index.md` and read only the one or two process
   details that change that decision.
3. Read `references/presentation-model.md`. Then read every retained source
   through the same decision dimensions: content structure, attention path,
   relation to its audience, hierarchy, rhythm and density, layout,
   typography, semantic color roles, materiality, imagery, motion, interaction
   posture, production conditions, and historical meaning. For each source
   record:
   - what concrete effect is observed;
   - which underlying decision may produce it;
   - which decision it changes for this project;
   - what must not be copied; and
   - which contextual difference limits the analogy.
4. Compare sources instead of averaging them. Extract recurring relations,
   productive tensions, and meaningful disagreements. Form two or three
   genuinely different candidate directions, each with a positive generative
   stance and a negative boundary. Do not reduce them to mood adjectives or
   color swatches.
5. Put the candidates before the named human reviewer using comparable
   artifacts, fragments, or verbal cases. Ask what is preferred, rejected, or
   recombined and why. The agent may prepare the probe; it cannot infer a
   person's taste or accept the direction on their behalf.
6. Synthesize the chosen evidence into one **provisional project direction**:

```text
Project, audiences, and source boundary:
Source field and provenance:
Comparative readings and tensions:
Human preference evidence:
Provisional generative stance:
Perceptual relation to preserve:
Provisional visual language and cross-surface expression allocation, when material:
Smallest project invariants:
Dimensions intentionally left variable:
Negative boundary:
Reference decisions inherited, transformed, or rejected:
First representative cases:
Observation that would revise or reject this direction:
Status, owner, and review date:
```

7. Store the references, preference evidence, and direction in the project's
   owning design area. Downstream cases may cite the direction but must still
   adapt it to their own audience and function. Promote it from provisional to
   accepted only after representative real artifacts and named human review;
   record contrary evidence rather than editing history into consensus.

The result is the project's first organizing visual proposition, not a finished
brand book. A provisional visual language may guide later agents, but tokens,
components, illustration rules, logo derivatives, and motion language arise
only where later design practice proves they are needed.
