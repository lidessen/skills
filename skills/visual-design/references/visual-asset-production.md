# Production Visual Assets

Use this reference only when a logo, icon, illustration, repeated scene, or
motion asset is a primary production object. It is not a generic image prompt,
style menu, asset registry, or requirement to generate alternatives for every
interface image.

The `visual-design` skill owns direction, comparison, system relation, and
review evidence. An available image, drawing, animation, or browser capability
owns execution. The project's asset pipeline owns formats and derivatives. The
designated human owns aesthetic acceptance and promotion of a canonical asset.

## Establish the production role

Before generating or editing, name only the conditions that can change the
asset decision:

```text
Asset role and audience action:
Owning direction or provisional visual language:
Surfaces and states that consume it:
Smallest and largest consequential use:
Background, crop, and theme conditions:
Functional meaning or decorative status:
Required static, motion, and reduced-motion forms:
Source, license, and reference boundaries:
Available production and inspection capabilities:
```

A decorative asset must not become the only carrier of required meaning. A
functional icon must retain a usable label or established accessible name. Do
not infer a format, resolution, color mode, or animation technology before the
actual delivery surface requires it.

## Read references as decisions

When a user or project supplies a visual reference, separate direct observation
from inferred effect. Inspect only dimensions that can change this production
decision, such as:

- silhouette and occupied area;
- contour weight, edge character, and geometric regularity;
- positive and negative mass;
- detail density at the target size;
- palette roles and contrast rather than copied values;
- materiality, texture, and reproduction behavior;
- composition, crop, and relation to surrounding type or controls; and
- temporal order, easing character, and settled state when motion is involved.

For each retained reference, record:

```text
Directly observed form:
Inferred perceptual effect:
Decision changed for this project:
Project-specific transformation:
What must not be copied:
Contextual or licensing limit:
```

Resemblance alone is not a decision. Do not reconstruct another product's
character, mascot, logo, proprietary asset, or distinctive composition. Use a
reference to expose a relation, then regenerate that relation from the current
project's identity and production conditions.

## Compare candidates under the same conditions

Create alternatives only when a material direction or production question is
still unresolved. Keep the comparison small enough for the human reviewer to
judge and vary a named relation rather than changing every property at once.

Compare candidates in the same representative matrix:

- consequential target sizes, including the smallest real use;
- actual light, dark, transparent, colored, or photographic backgrounds;
- normal crop, mask, and neighboring typography or interface context;
- ordinary and high-density displays when raster output makes that relevant;
- still, initial, transition, settled, and reduced-motion states when animated;
  and
- the most expressive surface alongside one ordinary downstream surface.

Do not invent universal pixel thresholds. The declared production use selects
the matrix. A large review image cannot prove that a navigation mark, favicon,
inline icon, print asset, or motion state works.

## Preserve system relation without forcing sameness

Name the smallest invariants shared with the surrounding product: for example
mass, contour character, contrast role, material relation, motion order, or
interaction posture. Also name what may remain more expressive in the asset.

If the asset and product feel unrelated, test movement from both sides before
forcing one to imitate the other. Simplifying the asset and strengthening the
surrounding system may produce a more coherent relation than adding decoration
everywhere. Review ordinary data, reading, and control surfaces so expressive
identity does not consume task attention.

When a provisional visual-language guide exists, translate its relation and
formal tendencies into the asset's actual production role. Do not turn “hand-
drawn,” “retro,” or another cue into a texture pass applied to a stable logo or
icon. Test whether silhouette, construction, contour, mass, color role, or
motion must change, and preserve recognition and functional meaning where the
consumer matrix requires them.

## Promote deliberately

After human selection:

1. retain the accepted source or canonical master in the project's owning
   design area;
2. generate only the production derivatives required by real consumers;
3. keep superseded candidates outside runtime assets when preserving the review
   trail is decision-relevant;
4. remove obsolete production copies that could create competing authorities;
5. verify file weight, transparency or background behavior, crop, target-size
   legibility, and build inclusion on the actual delivery path; and
6. record the human acceptance, remaining uncertainty, and observation that
   would reopen the asset decision.

Passing export and build checks prove production integrity, not aesthetic
acceptance. A selected asset remains project-local evidence; it does not become
a portable style default.
